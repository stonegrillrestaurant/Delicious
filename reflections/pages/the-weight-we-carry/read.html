The Weight We Carry

The religious belief that keeps our mind from evolving.

By Ninox Antolihao · Stone Grill Press · Leyte, Philippines · First Edition 2026

We often move through our days believing that the heaviest burdens we carry are the tasks in front of us—the endless logistics, the obligations, and the physical demands of keeping our reality running. But the truth is, the most exhausting weight we bear is entirely invisible. It is the weight of our own unexamined mystical minds.

There is a terrifying trap waiting for all of us in the quiet moments of life. When the noise of the day fades and we are finally left alone with nothing to do, the mind does not power down. Instead, it turns inward. It rummages through past memories, projects future anxieties, and replays the chaotic frequencies of the people around us. In this silence, we often fall back into our most comfortable habits—even when those habits are a slow-acting poison.

We are creatures wired for survival, and our brains are fiercely efficient. When we face frustration, guilt, or conflict, our minds default to the emotional highways we have traveled a thousand times before. We let our emotions rule our systems. We let the ego take the wheel.

The ego is humanity’s great tragedy. It evolved thousands of years ago as a vital shield to keep us alive in a dangerous world. But today, the tool for survival has become the bullet that endangers our own lives. When our ego is threatened by a disagreement or a shift in our environment, it fires that bullet blindly, shattering relationships, stalling our growth, and destroying our internal peace.

Many people accept religion not because they have truly examined it, but because it is the familiar cup they were raised to drink from. Even when it poisons the mind with fear, guilt, and obedience, they mistake that conditioned reaction for faith, and eventually for who they are.

We drink the poison simply because the cup is familiar, and we mistake that reactive, chaotic energy for who we truly are.

Much of our mental exhaustion comes from rumination—the desperate, draining attempt to solve puzzles that have already been glued together. We grieve our past mistakes, and we feel guilty for leaving beliefs that once gave us comfort but slowly drained our spirit.

Yet the unchangeable past is not a sacred burden we must carry forever. It is a heavy rock in the chest. It restricts your breathing, clouds your vision, and leaves you with no energy to build your future. Healing begins when we finally learn to put it down.

Wisdom is the realization that you have the power to simply drop the rock.

When we accept that a situation has already happened and that we have no power to change it, we reclaim our cognitive bandwidth. We stop agonizing and start focusing purely on the solution. Walking away from the toxic frequencies of others is not a retreat; it is the ultimate act of self-preservation. It is the conscious choice to stop carrying a weight that was never yours to bear.

To truly drop this weight, we must recognize that much of our suffering comes from running obsolete software or believing a belief system designed by societies thousands of years ago. Physically, our brains are still deeply connected to the ancient survival patterns of our ancestors—wired to fear the unknown, cling to the tribe, and seek absolute clarity in a terrifying world.

For early humanity, the vast unknown was a threat to survival. To cope, we filled the gaps in our knowledge with myths, dogmas, and the gods we created to make sense of the universe. This was the ultimate cognitive shortcut—a collective safety net that allowed the ancient mind to stop worrying and power down.

Today, our environment has exponentially evolved, yet some people still cling to this belief. Many prefer the lazy comfort of religious mystery over the rigorous demands of scientific discovery. We defend tribal dogmas and react to abstract disagreements with primal survival instinct, crashing our own internal systems because we refuse to update our critical thinking. We remain carbon copies of an ancient mindset, terrified to step into the modern environment.

If we want to stop being passengers in our own minds, we must slowly shift our focus from theology to reality.

There is a profound difference between the comfort of mystery and the comfort of clarity. The comfort of theology requires you to stay in the dark, constantly at the mercy of an unknown you refuse to look at. But clarity demands that you turn on the light. It asks you to look directly at your fears, your legacy code, and your emotional loops.

The moment you understand how the system works—whether it is the biological trigger of your own ego or the logic of the world around you—it loses its power to control you.

When we redirect the chaotic energy of our untamed emotions into structured thought, logic, and reality, we shrink the mystery. We externalize the internal chaos, turning the shout of our emotions into systems that make our daily reality smoother.

Clarity is ultimately far more comfortable than mystery because it places the control back in your hands. You are no longer relying on the outdated survival mechanisms of the religious teachings of the past. You understand the system, and in doing so, you become the architect of your own peace—not as a copy of the past, but as a participant creator of the present.
