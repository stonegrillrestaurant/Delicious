The Paradox of God

A Solitude Reflection by Ninox Antolihao

A meditation on belief, truth, and the mystery that cannot be named.

Anyone who claims to know the full truth about God speaks from illusion. For how can the finite explain the infinite? How can a single mind, bound by language and time, contain something that has no edges, no beginning, no name?

To speak of God is already to limit what cannot be limited. Each word becomes a frame — and the moment you frame the infinite, you lose it. So we do not describe God. We only describe our experience of not knowing.

Belief is not truth.

Belief is the bridge we build across uncertainty — but truth is the ocean beneath it. We walk on belief because we fear the deep — the unknown, the silence, the mystery. When we cannot explain a thing, we give it God’s name — thunder, creation, consciousness, death. In doing so, we comfort ourselves not with understanding, but with closure.

To say “God did it” often means: “I do not understand it.” And that is fine — until we begin preaching our uncertainty as absolute truth.

Every culture creates God in its own image — gentle or wrathful, distant or intimate — because the idea of God mirrors the human condition. We fear the unknown, so we name it. We crave meaning, so we imagine a giver of meaning. We suffer, so we imagine a healer.

But what if God is not someone we can describe, but the very space that allows all descriptions to exist?

To admit we do not understand God is not weakness — it is honesty. And honesty is where wisdom begins. The more tightly we cling to our definitions, the farther we move from truth. Maybe silence is the only sacred language left — for silence never lies.

In silence, we do not define God. We listen to the mystery.

The wisest thing ever said about God may simply be the confession: I do not know.
