Theology as a Barrier to Truth

When language becomes too divine, it can sometimes lose its human meaning.

By Ninox Antolihao Philosophical Reflection First Edition 2026
Listen to this reflection

Theology can act as a barrier to truth rather than a bridge, especially when it overcomplicates the raw, visceral reality of the human experience.

When we elevate human concepts to an absolute level, we are often no longer touching truth directly. We may only be interacting with a God shaped by our own vocabulary, a divine image built from the limits of human language, fear, longing, and imagination.

This does not mean that every theological idea is empty. But it does mean that theology must be handled carefully. The moment human words are stretched too far beyond their grounded meaning, they can begin to distort the very reality they were supposed to clarify.

The Distortion of Simple Words

Words like father, justice, and love have clear, grounded meanings in everyday human life. They are not abstract decorations. They are built on tangible actions, relationships, responsibilities, and emotional consequences.

A father protects. Justice restores balance. Love cares for the suffering presence in front of it. These meanings are not difficult to understand when they remain close to human experience.

But when theology takes these words and stretches them to describe an infinite, incomprehensible being, their definitions begin to fracture.

If a person is told that divine justice includes eternal punishment, or that a divine father allows immense suffering for a mysterious greater good, the ordinary meaning of those words becomes unstable. The mind is forced to accept a contradiction: that love may look like abandonment, that justice may look like endless pain, and that fatherhood may include silence in the face of suffering.

This reveals the profound impact of language on human thought. Once sacred vocabulary is placed beyond questioning, people may begin to lose the ability to recognize simple empathy and fairness in the real world.

Instead of clarifying reality, theology can muddy the waters, making it harder for people to practice simple, functional compassion.

The Tragedy of Over-Spiritualizing Human Pain

The cry, “My God, why have you forsaken me?” is one of the clearest examples of this hindrance.

At its core, this is a universal human expression. It is the ultimate cry of solitude, despair, and the terrifying feeling of being entirely alone in the face of suffering. It is a moment of pure, agonizing vulnerability that anyone who has ever experienced deep trauma can understand.

But what happens when theology gets hold of it?

Instead of allowing that moment to remain a profound reflection on the tragedy of the human condition, theology often rushes to solve it. It wraps the cry in complex doctrines of atonement, divine abandonment, cosmic legal transactions, and sacred necessity.

The simple, bleeding reality of a person feeling abandoned is buried under centuries of academic and dogmatic debate.

We project our own need for a neat and purposeful universe onto a chaotic moment. By turning a cry of despair into a preordained divine chess move, theology can shield us from having to sit with the uncomfortable reality of unvarnished suffering.

Hindering True Connection

When we cannot let a simple cry of pain just be a cry of pain, we hinder our ability to understand each other.

If everything must have a complex supernatural explanation, we lose touch with the shared, grounded reality of being human. We stop listening to the actual pain inside the words and start listening only to the doctrine we have attached to them.

A wounded person does not always need interpretation. Sometimes, the wounded person simply needs presence.

But theology often teaches people to translate pain too quickly. Instead of saying, “I hear you,” it says, “There is a hidden reason.” Instead of sitting beside grief, it tries to place grief inside a cosmic system.

The result is distance. The sufferer becomes an example. The wound becomes a lesson. The person disappears behind the explanation.

The Clarity of the Unfiltered Experience

When we finally let go of the need to elevate every experience into a cosmic event, we are left with the stark, beautiful, and sometimes terrifying reality of simply being human.

By stripping away the complex language and the intricate layers of the divine that human minds have constructed over millennia, we begin to dismantle the barriers between ourselves and reality.

Much of what we call theology is ultimately an elaborate framework built to make sense of the unknown. It is humanity trying to give shape to mystery. But when that framework becomes too rigid, it stops helping us see. It begins telling us what we are allowed to see.

When we step outside of that constructed reality, we stop trying to force human pain, joy, and justice into a supernatural mold. A cry of pain becomes exactly that: a cry that demands immediate human empathy, not a theological riddle that requires interpretation.

Without the constant reassurance of a dogmatic safety net, we are forced to sit with the true weight of our existence.

The Grounding Power of Solitude

This can bring deep, quiet moments of solitude. In those moments, we face the starkness of our own reality. We realize that we are fragile, finite, wounded, hopeful, confused, and alive.

Yet this solitude does not have to become despair. It can become grounding.

The realization that we are all navigating this finite existence together is not a weakness. It is the very thing that connects us. When we stop looking upward for a transcendent meaning that explains everything, we may finally begin looking across at each other.

If there is no grand divine chessboard to excuse or explain away suffering, then the responsibility to alleviate that suffering falls on our own shoulders.

That responsibility may feel heavy. But it is also honest.

The Freedom of Being Human

There is immense freedom in realizing that we do not need absolute, complex definitions to validate our lives.

The human condition, with all its flaws, fleeting moments, and raw emotions, is entirely sufficient on its own. We do not need to turn every wound into doctrine, every tear into destiny, or every silence into divine intention.

Sometimes pain is pain. Love is love. Abandonment is abandonment. Mercy is mercy.

And perhaps truth begins not when we explain these things away, but when we allow them to stand before us without disguise.

When theology becomes too complex, it can distance us from the truth of being human. But when we return to the raw experience itself, we recover something sacred in a different way: not sacred because it is beyond us, but sacred because it is honestly within us.

— ❖ —

To be human is already enough to be worthy of attention, compassion, and truth.
